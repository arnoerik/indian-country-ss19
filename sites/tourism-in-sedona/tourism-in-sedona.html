Native Americans in Tourism: How the Navajo, Hopi, and Hualapai Tribes have Reacted to Tourism in Arizona

By: Erikka Arno

Introduction

This page attempts to understand the sovereignty of tourist sites, infrastructure, economic and marketing issues, cultural and spiritual appropriation of Native Americans. I delve into how Native American peoples and their land is viewed and treated within the construction of these grand tourist spaces on indigenous lands. This page seeks to understand how the Navajo, Hopi, and Hualapai tribes have been affected by the explosion of tourism and, more importantly, their agency within. And how the Native tourism sectors interact with the Arizona Travel and Tourism Sector. I will specifically be investigating the state of Arizona at Sedona and the Grand Canyon. I will be analyzing Sedona’s tourism through the lens of the New Age movement that came about in the 1970’s.


The Navajo and Hopi Tribes


The Navajo reservation extends across northeastern Arizona, northwestern New Mexico, southeastern Utah and encompasses 27,000 square miles of land. The Navajo own the largest reservation in North America with a population around 350,000 people. The capital of the Navajo Nation is Window Rock, Arizona and encompasses an area of approximately six square miles. The Navajo Nation is well known for their beautiful red landscape of mountains, plateaus, and the Grand Canyon. The Navajo Tourism Department , based of of Window Rock, has five staff that oversee administration, marketing and project development. The Hopi tribe is a sovereign nation that is located in northeastern Arizona just east of the Grand Canyon.They are the westmost Pueblo Indian Tribe. The reservation is based within the Navajo Nation and Coconino county. The reservation makes up over 1.5 million acres of land. There are twelve villages in the Hopi towns spread over three mesas. Their reservation capital is Kykotsmovi Village.

What does New Age mean and where did it come from?

The official definition of New Age is an eclectic group of cultural attitudes arising in the late twentieth century Western society that are adopted from a variety of ancient and modern cultures, that emphasize beliefs
(such as reincarnation, holism, pantheism, and occultism) outside the mainstream, and that advance alternative approaches to spirituality, right living, and health (Merriam-Webster). New Age is also often described as a movement that advertises the possibility of an age of light and love through self transformation and healing. There are practices of shamanism, goddess worship, Eastern religions, crystal energies, pagan rituals, extraterrestrials and channeling spirit beings.United States Native American is of very popular interest, especially among Native Americans located in the southwestern U.S.

In many scholarly writings the New Age movement is attributed to postmodern culture of consumer capitalism. Scholar Lisa Aldred argues that the “baby boomer” generation feel disconnected from any
cultural traditions and religion. Therefore, they seek spiritual significance elsewhere which catalyzed the New Age movement. Native American spirituality is the most popular and profitable sectors of New Age commercialization. They consume products such as crystals, self-help books, dream catchers, Native American spiritual workshops , meditation retreats, etc. However, engrossing these products cannot be enough to create a true ‘culture’. People who identify as New Age are a group with no history, traditions, community or meaning.

The most important aspects of this movement are: the focus on self and ‘self transformation’, the search for authenticity, and the centralization of spirituality. These are important to understand the impact this has on Native American culture while also understanding what meaning it holds to New agers.

    Impact of the New Age Movement on Native Americans: How does it affect them personally and within the tourism industry?


    Sedona, Arizona is full of natural rustic red landscape. With mountains, plateaus, the Red Rocks, the Grand Canyon, etc. It’s always had its place among popular tourist spots. But the largely imagined Native presence is implored by the followers of the New Age movement and imposed ancient wisdom, strong Native spirituality and mythical connections onto the people and the landscape as well. The Arizona tourism industry saw this ‘mystical’ enticement and quickly started using the same kind of language in advertising brochures which perpetuated this contrived Native American identity and the apprehension of it. The people who run these healing retreats and Native spirituality workshops use and reference real Hopi and Navajo mythology. In a chapter by Curtis Coats he describes the interaction he had on a tour in Sedona. A man named Thomas led the group in a medicine wheel ceremony. He spoke native language followed by English translations and completed the releasing ceremony with a shamanistic herb blend. Being with and sharing with the indigenous other comes from perceived genuine authenticity of watching Native dance, participating in ceremonies and walking on sacred ground with the indigenous Other (Coats 2007).
