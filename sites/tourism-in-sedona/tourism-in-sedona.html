Native Americans in Tourism: How the Navajo, Hopi, and Hualapai Tribes have Reacted to Tourism in Arizona

Introduction

This page attempts to understand the sovereignty of tourist sites, infrastructure, economic and marketing issues, cultural and spiritual appropriation of Native Americans. I delve into how Native American peoples and their land is viewed and treated within the construction of these grand tourist spaces on indigenous lands. This page seeks to understand how the Navajo, Hopi, and Hualapai tribes have been affected by the explosion of tourism and, more importantly, their agency within. And how the Native tourism sectors interact with the Arizona Travel and Tourism Sector. I will specifically be investigating the state of Arizona at Sedona and the Grand Canyon. I will be analyzing Sedona’s tourism through the lens of the New Age movement that came about in the 1970’s.


The Navajo and Hopi Tribes


The Navajo reservation extends across northeastern Arizona, northwestern New Mexico, southeastern Utah and encompasses 27,000 square miles of land. The Navajo own the largest reservation in North America with a population around 350,000 people. The capital of the Navajo Nation is Window Rock, Arizona and encompasses an area of approximately six square miles. The Navajo Nation is well known for their beautiful red landscape of mountains, plateaus, and the Grand Canyon. The Navajo Tourism Department , based out of Window Rock, has five staff that oversee administration, marketing and project development. The Hopi tribe is a sovereign nation that is located in northeastern Arizona just east of the Grand Canyon.They are the westmost Pueblo Indian Tribe. The reservation is based within the Navajo Nation and Coconino county. The reservation makes up over 1.5 million acres of land. There are twelve villages in the Hopi towns spread over three mesas. Their reservation capital is Kykotsmovi Village.

    Impact of the New Age Movement on Native Americans: How does it affect them personally and within the tourism industry?


    Sedona, Arizona is full of natural rustic red landscape. With mountains, plateaus, the Red Rocks, the Grand Canyon, etc. It’s always had its place among popular tourist spots. But the largely imagined Native presence is implored by the followers of the New Age movement and imposed ancient wisdom, strong Native spirituality and mythical connections onto the people and the landscape as well. The Arizona tourism industry saw this ‘mystical’ enticement started using the same kind of language in advertising brochures which perpetuated this contrived Native American identity. The people who own craft shops in Sedona often sell "ceremonial supplies", such as: turkey feathers, sage smudgesticks, palo santo, etc. These items can promote the commodification of Native American spirituality. It contrast, this would be along the same lines of someone selling the challic for wine and communion bread used in Catholic churches. Native Americans need much more economic support to get truly involved in the tourism/travel industry. This comes down to better communication between indigenous and the non-indigenous communities and their involvement with each other.


    Navajo, Hopi, and Hualapi Sovereignty Over Tourism

    Despite the rise of the New Age movement in the past three decades that has appropriated Native American culture and romanticized the image of the ‘simple, ancient shaman’ , Native American agency over tourism is looking up. Evidence from the Hualapai owned skywalkway that opened in 2007. Although, there are plenty of shops that do buy authentic products direct from Hopi and Navajo artists there seem to be no shops actually owned by the Hopi or Navajo people in Sedona despite their products being sold everywhere. This comes down to better communication between indigenous and the non-indigenous communities, their involvement and development with each other. The Indian Arts and Crafts board is important, especially in the Native American tourism industry. The board was established when the Indian Arts and Craft Act was passed in 1990. This act is a truth-advertising law that protects the authenticity of Native American products. It prohibits the misrepresentation in the marketing of Indian arts and crafts. A product can’t be on sale that might suggest its Indian produced when it is not. The American Indian Alaskan Native Tourists Association is helping tribal organizations engage in cultural tourism. This organization help grows tribal tourism through technical assistance, providing education resources, and training.
